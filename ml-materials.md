<section class="section-block">
  <h1>Gentle ML Introductions and Tutorials</h1>
  <p>
    A curated list of online materials I enjoyed for learning machine learning concepts with clear intuition. You can use this as a refresher if you already learned it in the past or as a primer if you are about to get into it. At the end of the day, practicing problem with an actual book or class is still the best way to "make it stick", but I have enjoyed listening to some of these on my free time.
  </p>
</section>

      <h2>Friendly Foundations</h2>
      <ul>
        <li><a href="https://www.3blue1brown.com/topics/neural-networks" target="_blank" rel="noopener">3Blue1Brown: Neural Networks</a> - visual intuition first.</li>
        <li><a href="https://www.3blue1brown.com/topics/differential-equations" target="_blank" rel="noopener">3Blue1Brown: Differential Equations</a> - visual intuition first.</li>
        <li><a href="https://www.statquest.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">StatQuest</a> - simple explanations for core ML and statistics ideas.</li>
        <li><a href="https://huggingface.co/spaces/lbourdois/SSM_blog_posts" target="_blank" rel="noopener">State Space Models</a> - A concise and effective introduction to state space models. I am generally a fan of HuggingFace in general as plataform!</li>
      </ul>
    </article>

    <article class="card">
      <h2>Classes/Tutorials</h2>
      <ul>
        <li><a href="https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/18-03-differential-equations-spring-2010/video_galleries/video-lectures/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">MIT 18.03 - Differential Equations</a> - I never took a differential equations class. For a working knowledge of the topic there are three takeaways that made this lecture series worth it. First, a lot of ODEs revert to pretty formulaic formulas and motifs, so knowing that there is a straighforward way to solve them is useful, especially if you are reading more principled AI papers in things like dynamical systems or diffusions. Second, Having a good intuition and technical understanding of things like Fourier and Laplace transforms is very useful to converse with the the engineering world. I never thought I'd care about it that much, but getting interested in AI for world models and physical systems comes with the burden of needing a good understanding of this heavy machinery.</li>
        <li><a href="https://cs229.stanford.edu/materials.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Stanford CS229 Materials</a> - classic notes for fundamentals.</li>
        <li><a href="https://www.deeplearning.ai/courses/deep-learning-specialization/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">deeplearning.ai Deep Learning Specialization</a> - If you are like me, you might enjoy classes that are more theory heavy, where you get to develop intuition and write proofs on pen and paper. The drawback is that on a day-to-day, you actually need to know how to move those ideas to working code. Most deeplearning.ai courses are very good at explaining meaningful architectural details and giving targeted practice that you might not get in a regular classroom.</li>
        <li><a href="https://uvadlc-notebooks.readthedocs.io/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">UVA Deep Learning Course Notebooks</a> - I have used this a little bit to start understanding JAX better and it is pretty well written and mostly self-contained. It also has equivalent and clear PyTorch tutorials.</li>
      </ul>
    </article>
